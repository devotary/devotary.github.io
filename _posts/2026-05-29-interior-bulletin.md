The first tour through the reopened wing was almost persuasive. Fresh paint. New hallway plates. Clean glass in frames that still smelled of packing paper. Then the guide skipped offices 2B through 2F without comment and accelerated the group straight toward the conference room, where a ceremonial packet described a successful handover and urged all personnel to respect sealed workspaces during transition.

Maintenance tickets tell a different story. Several work orders mention cable pulls and reinforced interior frames in rooms that were supposedly untouched. A janitor's routing sheet, later withdrawn, listed the same corridor under restricted access three separate times in one week. No one has explained why an area ready for ribbon cutting still required blackout film on the inner windows.

> "You can tell what matters by what gets called temporary for too long." — overheard during the reopening walk-through

## What the brief omitted

Most of the briefing packet was boilerplate, but one line stood out: all staff were instructed to use the southern stairwell until further notice because the lift calibration remained incomplete. The lift calibration order, however, had been signed off four days earlier. That would be easy to ignore if the southern stairwell did not also bypass the most heavily locked segment of the floor.

<div class="ornament">* * *</div>

For now, the administrative wing reads less like a completed transfer and more like a stage set built around a hidden job already in progress. People entering the space should pay attention to who carries keys, who carries clipboards, and which doors are always just about to be opened later.
