By first light, the posted schedules on pier three no longer matched the schedules copied into the quarter office ledger. Nobody admitted making the changes. Nobody admitted noticing them, either, until a patrol clerk began comparing chalk marks against the typed roster and discovered that three deliveries had been moved into windows when the watch tower was known to be empty.

The explanation offered at breakfast was routine congestion. That story held for eight minutes. It collapsed the moment someone realized the missing fuel drums were all from the same bonded stack and all of them had been signed out under contractor tags that do not exist on the current registry. The dockmaster denied forging the entries. The registry clerk denied ever seeing the names before. The tug pilots, when asked, became suddenly interested in weather patterns.

> "If it were theft, it would be messy. This looks more like someone expected us not to compare copies." — unsigned note left in the harbor office inbox

## Pattern, not accident

The more useful detail is not what went missing but what stayed perfectly in place. Spare lines, loading hooks, sealed manifests, and public-facing traffic sheets all remained tidy enough to reassure anyone skimming the room. Only the operational layers underneath had been touched. That suggests a reader who knew exactly which records mattered and which ones existed mainly for appearances.

<div class="ornament">* * *</div>

By noon the eastern slips were running again, outwardly normal, with a revised guard rotation and a brand new insistence that all duplicate schedules be surrendered for shredding. That request was ignored by at least one clerk, which is why this issue exists. Readers are advised to pay closer attention to the storage yards inland from the cranes. When the paper trail gets cleaned too quickly, the traffic usually moves somewhere less obvious.
