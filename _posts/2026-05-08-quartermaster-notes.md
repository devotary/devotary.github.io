Warehouse B reported eighteen sealed equipment crates on Monday morning. By Tuesday afternoon, the same manifest showed fifteen, with the missing three reappearing on a transfer sheet addressed to a yard that had not requested them. By Wednesday, all paperwork had been "corrected" to reflect a routine redistribution. The trouble with routine redistribution is that it generally leaves witnesses who remember carrying the boxes.

The quartermaster's office prefers uncertainty that sounds administrative. Bad handwriting. Smudged stamps. Similar crate numbers. Those excuses work right up until you compare them with access logs and discover the same small cluster of names appearing near every disputed transfer. None of this proves intent by itself, but it does prove that the inventory drift is not random enough to dismiss.

## Watch the edges

Readers who handle logistics already know the center of a supply chain is usually honest. The manipulation happens at the edges: receiving, relabeling, temporary staging, and the quiet handoff between responsibility tables. That is where crates become stories, and stories become missing items nobody can place on the right form by the end of the week.

<div class="ornament">* * *</div>

Until the counts stabilize, assume every tidy manifest reflects a contested process upstream. Count twice. Copy once. Keep the copy. The office that wants your original most urgently is usually the office worth reading most closely.
